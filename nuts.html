WALNUTS

Walnuts are the edible seeds of trees belonging to the Juglans genus, with the most common variety being the English walnut (Juglans regia). Encased in a hard, ridged shell, the walnut kernel has a unique, brain-like appearance and a mildly bitter, nutty flavor. Revered since ancient times, walnuts have been a staple in both Eastern and Western diets for their rich taste and impressive health properties.

They are particularly rich in omega-3 fatty acids (ALA), which support heart and brain health. Walnuts are widely celebrated for their brain-boosting and heart-protective properties. The combination of omega-3s, polyphenols, and antioxidants helps reduce inflammation, improve blood lipid profiles, and support cognitive function. Studies suggest that regular walnut consumption may help lower the risk of heart disease, enhance memory, and even reduce symptoms of depression and age-related cognitive decline.

Walnuts are incredibly versatile in the kitchen. They can be enjoyed raw, roasted, or toasted, and are often used in both sweet and savory dishes. Common culinary applications include baked goods (like brownies, banana bread, and cookies), salads, granolas, and pesto sauces. Ground walnuts are also used in Middle Eastern and Mediterranean cuisine to thicken sauces or create fillings for pastries and savory rolls.

To maintain freshness and prevent rancidity, walnuts should be stored in an airtight container in the refrigerator or freezer, especially after being shelled. When buying, opt for unsalted, raw, or dry-roasted varieties without added oils or preservatives. Their rich taste, crunchy texture, and superior health benefits make walnuts a smart, everyday superfood choice.
